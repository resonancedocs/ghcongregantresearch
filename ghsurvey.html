Overall, respondents are highly educated and married. 65% were white, while 23% were Black or African American. 5% chose two or more races. 60% reported attending every week, 26% answered two or three times a month, and 8% said monthly or less. Respondents tend to overestimate how much they actually attend, but this information is still helpful in understanding how they see themselves and their relationship to the church.

The tenure at Greenhouse was evenly distributed: 26% have attended 10+ years, 26% have been there 1–3 years, and 21% are in the 7–10 year group.

Implication
Word of mouth has essentially been the entire growth engine.
Very few people are finding Greenhouse through social media or Google — which means the digital front door is wide open.
The opportunities are two-fold:
1. Continue to lean into word of mouth and invitations.
2. The fact that very few people are finding Greenhouse through social media or SEO strategies is a significant opportunity and a new pathway to fuel Greenhouse's growth.

Respondents 25–54 expressed difficulty finding community. This group said they were very likely to engage more if more community events were added. Interestingly, people who completed Activate did not score meaningfully higher on community/friendship than those who didn't.
The 25–54 cohort is the most motivated but the least satisfied with what's available. They have the highest event desire, solid Activate completion, and strong attendance — but community scores drag. They're doing everything "right" by the church's system, and still feel somewhat disconnected.

What People Are Asking For
01 Gender-specific ministry — women's retreats, Bible studies, men's events
02 Marriage & couples retreats — "Marriage in May" mourned by multiple respondents
03 Singles ministry for all ages — 20s, 40s, 50s, 60s all explicitly requested it
04 Microchurch restructure — forced multiplication, no safety net when it fails
05 Family ministry — life-stage microchurches, childcare support for couples
06 Communication upgrade — events calendar, email follow-up, staff responsiveness
Key Strategic Takeaways
The friendship gap is real.
The microchurch model has worked for some, but many have not found the community they hoped for — with many giving up. They want more connection opportunities.
The opportunity for growth through communication is significant.
Word of mouth has essentially been the entire growth engine. Communication systems need a real upgrade — this came up too consistently to dismiss. It's not about social media strategy; it's about basic responsiveness, a functional events calendar, and following up with people who reach out.
A system problem, not a people problem.
The 25–54 cohort is doing everything right by the church's system — attending, completing Activate, joining Microchurch — and still feel disconnected. That's not on them.
