Greenhouse Church  ·  Focus Group Report  ·  December 2025
Focus Group Overview
20 total participants across
two focus groups of 10
17 women participants  ·  average age
late 30s, ranging early 20s to late 50s
2 balanced groups — half attending
< 3 years, half 3+ years
These conversations were designed to surface consistent themes around why people attend, what they value most, and where friction or gaps exist in their experience. Each group was intentionally balanced between newer and more established attendees to provide perspective across different stages of belonging.
Reason 01
Kids & Youth Ministries
Parents describe kids and youth ministry as one of the clearest reasons they remain engaged at Greenhouse. The value is not limited to childcare or programming. Congregants point to visible spiritual formation: children learning Scripture, talking about Jesus at home, enjoying church, and growing in their own walk with God. For parents, especially mothers, the strength of kids and youth ministry creates confidence that Greenhouse is not only serving adults on Sunday, but helping form their children in ways they can see and name.
Exact Quotes
"The thing that keeps me staying here is kids ministry... I think that children's ministry has really evolved since I got here."
"My son said that he learns a lot about Jesus. And I noticed a few different things because he's always been insured. He started to bring in his Bible."
"For me, having a place for my kids coming and having fun, but also growing in their walk, where my daughter's telling me what she's learning... it's actually changing them. That's huge for me as a mom because it's not every church."
"They do a really good job of breaking the word down in a way that they understand it... they're very fun with it, but also teaching the word in a way that it sticks."
Reason 02
Trust in Leadership & Teaching Stability
Congregants describe trust in leadership as a major stabilizing factor. Their confidence is not rooted in predictability, personality, or agreement on every issue, but in the perception that teaching is consistently anchored in Scripture. That creates security for people who have been cautious or wounded in other church environments. Even when congregants are unsure where leadership will land, they express confidence that the direction will be shaped by the Bible rather than pressure, trend, or personal preference.
Exact Quotes
"He's not predictable. And the only way to predict what he's going to do or what side he's going to land on about any number of issues, in my opinion, is scripture."
"In the rare circumstance where I am not sure about an issue, I tend to feel confident in his leadership in those areas, whereas in the past, with other pastors that I've been under, I'm very much like, wait, wait, pause."
"Mike is preaching Bible. Matt, Byron, whomever goes up — they are preaching the life."
Reason 03
Alignment with Vision, Theology, and Values
People are drawn to Greenhouse because the mission is repeated clearly and consistently. Congregants do not describe the church as merely offering services or programs. They describe a church with a defined theological and missional center: making disciples, giving people freedom to respond to their calling, and creating space for Spirit-led ministry without excessive control. The repetition of the mission builds confidence because people hear the same purpose reinforced often enough to believe the church is serious about it.
Exact Quotes
"I love the fact that the mission of the church is stated all the time. With any good organization, to repeat the mission over and over and over again — that's how people know that you're serious."
"There's a freedom here to allow people to be who they are called to be... there's good leadership, but yet they're not controlled."
"Just the mission of making disciples — it's just a passion of mine, to see people come to know Jesus. And I just love the fact that that mission is just repeated."
Reason 04
Sunday Worship & Teaching Experience
The Sunday experience matters because congregants encounter both spiritual depth and practical teaching. Worship is described as anointed, humble, and non-performative, while teaching is described as both theological and applicable. For some, the experience of the Holy Spirit was decisive in their decision to stay. The Sunday gathering is not only a weekly service for these congregants; it is the moment where they sense spiritual authenticity, receive direction, and feel sustained for the week ahead.
Exact Quotes
"This church has more talent, more anointing than that church ever had. The humility of the people and the desire to truly worship and not perform is so incredible."
"It's difficult to marry theological information and the practical outcomes of it. Usually, you get one or the other. Mike makes people who've been a part of a church for a long time consider things differently."
"When I came here, not wanting to come here, I felt the Holy Spirit speaking to me when I got here. So I knew I was gonna be here."
Reason 05
Individual Leaders Who Care
Connection often happens because a specific person notices, pursues, and personally helps someone find a place. Congregants repeatedly describe breakthrough moments that were not produced by a system alone, but by an individual who saw confusion, absence, or disconnection and acted. These stories show that relational ownership at the individual level can change someone's entire experience of the church.
Exact Quotes
"I didn't know Janine. I was walking in the lobby one day... looking so confused that she came up to me and said, 'Well, what do you want?' That's how I got plugged in."
"There are certain individuals who just came and scooped us up and said, 'You don't feel connected? How can we help you? Let me connect you here, let me connect you here.' Sometimes just one person will change someone's complete experience."
"She reached out to me. The very first thing she said? 'Oh my God, it's so good to know that I was missed.' People don't want to be seen until they're missed."
Reason 06
Missional Generosity
Greenhouse's approach to generosity builds trust because congregants experience it as non-manipulative and consistent. People appreciate that giving is not pressured, especially for first-time guests, which makes them more comfortable inviting others. The commitment to giving away a significant portion of resources also reinforces the belief that the church is acting from conviction rather than institutional self-protection.
Exact Quotes
"I've never heard, in my years here, 'you gotta give this' or 'we gonna take up extra money this Sunday.' Your first time here, don't even give. We don't want your money. This is what they say."
"It makes me feel comfortable that I can bring someone I know — during the offering it's not going to be awkward."
"Whatever it is, whatever the Lord said — 50% — then it's 50. And that's just it. I like that he's consistent with the fact that the Word's head."
Reason 07
Healthier Than Past Churches
Several congregants compare Greenhouse favorably to prior church experiences. They describe it as healthier, more Spirit-led, less financially driven, and more diverse. These comparisons matter because many attendees are not evaluating Greenhouse in isolation. They are measuring it against previous church environments where they experienced rigidity, financial pressure, limited diversity, or lack of spiritual responsiveness.
Exact Quotes
"This church is much more healthier than the previous one."
"I felt like the other churches had a lot of great qualities, but for me it was the spirit-led portion — the church was interruptible if they felt like God was speaking."
"I came from a church where the emphasis has been finances first. Here, I've never heard that in my years here."
"One Sunday when I came to Greenhouse, and look at the room — all different races. The kingdom of God. I think he has a difficult task, and I think he handles it very well."
"It's not that we have everything perfect or agree 100%. But what I see is I love being with people who think different from me, and they teach me things I don't know."
Reason 08
Hope That Things Will Improve
Even when congregants name real frustrations, many remain because they still carry hope. Some have stayed through years of disconnection because the teaching sustained them, the church's values mattered to them, or they finally began to experience relational breakthrough. This hope is fragile but important. It shows that people are not simply complaining; they want Greenhouse to become the kind of church they believe it can be.
Exact Quotes
"For nine years of not feeling connected — it just took so long. But I finally feel like with certain people in the church, I broke through. So now I'm on the other side."
"Hearing the sermon was exactly what I needed at the time to sustain myself for the next week. It held out hope."
Problem 01
Difficulty Forming Real, Lasting Relationships
The clearest relational problem is not that people are unwilling to connect, but that connection is difficult to form and sustain. Congregants describe years of attending, meeting many people, and still struggling to find lasting relationships. The issue is especially significant because it affects both newer and long-time attendees. People can be present, serving, and even participating in microchurch while still feeling lonely.
Exact Quotes
"I've been here seven years, and we have met lots of people. There's one person I can think of in particular — she's been here 16 years. I just had lunch with her yesterday, and we still have the same conversation about how it's so hard to get connected."
"Greenhouse is just too big, no one's reaching out to me. But I had to go and keep asking people and keep sending emails. If you're trying to get people plugged in, that's not gonna work for everyone."
"I know people that are going to microchurch, but then you talk to them and they're lonely. They feel like 'I'm going to microchurch but I still don't feel connected throughout the week.' And then they think something's wrong with them."
Problem 02
Communication Breakdowns
Communication gaps are experienced most sharply when important moments are missed by people who are already engaged. Congregants describe serving, attending regularly, and still missing critical transitions or opportunities. This creates a trust problem because the issue is not lack of interest from the congregation. It is that information is not reliably reaching the people who need it.
Exact Quotes
"Me being here every single Sunday, me serving a couple times a month — we were unaware of a vital, important part of going from 5th to 6th grade, which we call launch camp. There was no announcement made. It just slipped through the cracks."
"The biggest misconception about communication is the delusion that it has taken place. They may say something somewhere, and because they said something somewhere, they think everybody heard it."
"They have about 6,000 people calling this their home church. In the email system, I think there's about 900 emails."
Problem 03
Microchurch Confusion & Relational Disruption
Microchurch is intended to create community, but congregants describe moments where the structure disrupted the very relationships it was meant to protect. Multiplication, unclear closure, and changing group dynamics created sadness, hurt, and hesitation to try again. The most painful comments come from people who had finally found a place to belong and then experienced that connection being broken.
Exact Quotes
"Our first microchurch — we got so connected and it was so amazing, it was so life-giving. And then it started multiplying, multiplying, multiplying... my husband had finally found people he could be himself with. And when that was removed, it's been a year-long sadness to watch."
"It took something that was really organic and made it too structured, where you lost this beautiful thing. People now that you could see blossoming kind of shut up to themselves and said, 'I don't want to do this again.'"
"I tried and then was like, oh yes — and even those were more painful. One microchurch said they were going to close down, and then months later I found out they never closed. They just never told me."
Problem 04
Over-Reliance on Microchurch as the Only Relational Path
Congregants do not reject microchurch. They question whether it can carry the full relational burden of the church by itself. Multiple comments point to a need for additional relational environments where people can meet outside their existing circles, especially through women's ministry, men's ministry, or broader gatherings. The concern is that when microchurch is the primary path, people who do not connect there have few other clear options.
Exact Quotes
"I think microchurches are great. I just think we put such an emphasis on microchurch here that there's some disconnect we're missing — for some reason it's not translating to people forming real, deep connection."
"I don't think Activate is related to microchurch. After doing that, I still have to figure out what microchurch I need to join. And if you don't have that initiative, you just stop there."
"I think they've had it many years ago — a women's ministry where once or twice a year it was open to women to actually meet people outside of your circle. Or a men's ministry where it's not just a microchurch, so you get to meet other men."
Problem 05
Onboarding & Next-Step Confusion
The next-step process requires more initiative than many people will naturally have. Congregants describe difficulty finding a microchurch, unclear information online, and a lack of differentiation between groups. The result is that motivated people may eventually find a place, but passive or overwhelmed people are likely to stop trying. The problem is not just information access; it is the lack of guided movement from interest to belonging.
Exact Quotes
"Trying to find a microchurch was difficult. The microchurch I'm in, I love — but I never would have picked it on my own. Even when my daughter and I were looking intently at the website, the information was just not there. You can't tell — there's nothing to differentiate any of them. Just names."
"People who are more passive will just go to other churches. I think it takes so much intentionality — you have to search for it, and after one try you can't give up. But a lot of people aren't like that. They want people to come to them."
"The average church attendee comes about once a month. Having a family come three consecutive weeks is very difficult when you have sports, family and commitments."
Problem 06
Men's Engagement Lag
Men's engagement appears to lag behind women's engagement, especially in relational connection and ongoing participation. Congregants describe husbands who attend or serve, but remain minimally engaged or relationally disconnected. When men do find genuine connection, the loss of that connection is deeply felt. This suggests that men may need more intentional, lower-friction pathways into friendship, belonging, and ownership.
Exact Quotes
"How does your husband's engagement level compare to yours?" [Response:] "5% or 10% of what I do."
"My husband is very introverted... he struggles with getting connected. He comes and serves and then... he's right there but not really engaged."
"My husband had finally found people where he could be himself, not have to be all spiritual and tough like a church person. He was like, 'These people are different. This is a great group.' And then when that was removed, it's like it's been a year-long sadness."
Problem 07
Staff & Volunteer Burnout Concerns
Congregants are aware that Greenhouse is carrying a large vision with significant pressure on staff and volunteers. Their concern is not resistance to the mission, but fear that growth, generosity, and high ministry output may outpace the resources needed to sustain people well. Burnout is seen as a risk to staff health, volunteer joy, and the long-term ability of the church to continue its mission.
Exact Quotes
"My fear at Greenhouse is there's so much to do. It's so task-oriented and not process-oriented. You do tasks, you don't have time — you just check it and go to the next thing. My fear is will it grow to the point that staff is too much, overwhelmed, and burnt out, and then it will flap."
"I love that we give 50%. But I also want to make sure we don't give so much that our staff is not able to do things or we cannot grow. Then all missions stop."
"On the worship team, we don't even have a worship director. We now have these groups of volunteers saying, 'Okay, we are filling the gap.' Which is an honor, but it is a lot for a huge church like this."
"People were getting burnt out just from the sheer amount of things that we do. It was hard to find people to volunteer because people were like, 'No — I'm back here every week.' We need resources to come behind people so there's still joy in doing it."
